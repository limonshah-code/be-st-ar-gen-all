## What Does It Mean When A German Shepherd Has A Long Coat?

Have you ever seen a German Shepherd with flowing, luxurious fur and wondered if it was a different breed? Many people do. These dogs, known as long coat German Shepherds, are simply a variation of the standard German Shepherd breed. Their distinct appearance comes from a specific genetic trait.

A German Shepherd has a long coat due to a recessive gene. This gene causes their fur to grow longer than typical stock coat German Shepherds. This article explores what it means when a German Shepherd has a long coat, including their unique characteristics, grooming requirements, and standing within breed standards. We will discuss their genetics, care, and how they compare to other types of German Shepherds. Prepare to learn about these beautiful dogs.

## Understanding the Long Coat German Shepherd

A long coat German Shepherd is a purebred German Shepherd Dog. They have hair that is longer and often softer than the common stock coat. This coat type results from a specific recessive gene. These dogs are not a mixed breed or a separate breed; they are simply a variation within the German Shepherd lineage.

The long coat gives these German Shepherds a majestic appearance. Their fur can be wavy or straight and often forms feathering on their ears, legs, and tail. This longer hair often lacks the dense undercoat of a traditional stock coat. This means their insulation from extreme weather can differ. Understanding their genetics helps us appreciate this beautiful coat.

### The Genetic Basis for Long Coats

The presence of a long coat in a German Shepherd is genetic. It is controlled by a recessive gene, often referred to as the FGF5 gene. This means a puppy must inherit two copies of this gene, one from each parent, to express the long coat trait. If a dog inherits only one copy, they will be a carrier of the long coat gene but will have a stock coat.

Two stock coat German Shepherds can produce long coat puppies if both parents are carriers of the recessive gene. This explains why a long coat can appear unexpectedly in a litter. Breeders often test for this gene to predict coat types. Knowing the genetics helps breeders manage breeding programs. It ensures owners understand their dog's lineage.

### Distinguishing Long Coat from Stock Coat German Shepherds

The main difference between a long coat and a stock coat German Shepherd is the length and texture of their fur. Stock coat German Shepherds have a dense double coat with a shorter, harsher outer coat and a soft, thick undercoat. This provides excellent insulation. Their fur lies close to their body.

Long coat German Shepherds, by contrast, have significantly longer hair. They may or may not have an undercoat. If they do, it is usually less dense. This often makes their coat feel softer and more plush. The length of the fur is the defining characteristic. ## Grooming Needs for a Long Coat German Shepherd

Long coat German Shepherds require consistent and thorough grooming. Their long fur can easily tangle and mat if not cared for properly. Regular brushing is important. It prevents knots and removes loose hair. This also helps distribute natural oils through their coat.

I find that daily brushing works best for my long-haired dog. It keeps their coat looking good and reduces shedding around the house. Grooming sessions are a good time to bond with your dog. It also allows you to check for any skin issues or parasites. A clean coat is a healthy coat.

### Brushing and Dematting

Regular brushing is the cornerstone of long coat German Shepherd grooming. Use a pin brush, slicker brush, and a metal comb. These tools help get through the long fur. Start with a wide-toothed comb to gently loosen any tangles. Then use a slicker brush to remove loose fur.

Focus on areas prone to matting, like behind the ears, armpits, and under the tail. If you encounter a mat, gently work it out with your fingers or a dematting tool. Never pull or cut through mats carelessly. This can hurt your dog's skin. Consistent short sessions are better than infrequent long ones.

### Bathing and Drying

Bathing a long coat German Shepherd should be done as needed, typically every 4-6 weeks. Use a dog-specific shampoo and conditioner. Rinse thoroughly to prevent skin irritation. Leftover shampoo can cause dryness and itching.

Drying is just as important as washing. Their thick, long fur holds water. Use towels to absorb as much water as possible. Then, use a high-velocity dog dryer on a cool setting. Ensure the coat is completely dry to prevent skin infections and matting. Damp fur can lead to fungal growth. Remember, proper drying keeps their skin healthy.

### Seasonal Shedding and Coat Care

Long coat German Shepherds shed year-round, but they have two major shedding seasons. These typically occur in spring and fall. During these times, they "blow their coat," meaning they shed large amounts of fur. Increased brushing is necessary during these periods.

During heavy shedding, consider professional grooming services. They have powerful dryers and tools to remove dead undercoat effectively. Never shave a German Shepherd's coat. Shaving can damage their natural insulation. It also changes the coat texture permanently. ## Health Considerations for Long Coat German Shepherds

The long coat itself does not make a German Shepherd more prone to specific health issues beyond skin and coat care. However, like all German Shepherds, long coat variations can be susceptible to certain genetic health problems. Responsible breeders screen their dogs for these conditions. This helps ensure healthy puppies.

Overall health depends on good breeding practices and proper care. Providing a balanced diet, regular exercise, and routine vet check-ups keeps them strong. I always discuss my dog's specific needs with my veterinarian. This proactive approach helps manage their health effectively.

### Common German Shepherd Health Issues

German Shepherds, regardless of coat type, are prone to certain orthopedic conditions. Hip and elbow dysplasia are common. These conditions affect the joints. Good breeders screen parent dogs to reduce the risk. Early detection and management are important for a good quality of life.

Another concern is degenerative myelopathy (DM). This is a progressive neurological disease. It affects the spinal cord. It can lead to weakness and paralysis. Bloat, or gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV), is also a serious, life-threatening condition. Knowing these risks helps owners recognize symptoms early.

### Skin and Coat Health

While the long coat does not cause new health problems, it can make existing ones harder to spot. Skin irritations, hot spots, or parasites can hide beneath the thick fur. Regular grooming allows for skin checks. Look for redness, flakiness, or unusual lumps.

Their coat might offer less insulation against extreme cold or heat if they lack a dense undercoat. In very hot climates, they might feel the heat more. Provide plenty of shade and water. In cold climates, ensure they have proper shelter. A healthy coat reflects overall health.

## Long Coat German Shepherds in Breed Standards

The recognition of long coat German Shepherds in breed standards varies. The American Kennel Club (AKC) considers a long coat a fault in the show ring. This means dogs with long coats cannot compete in conformation shows in the AKC. They are still recognized as purebred, just not as desirable for show purposes.

Other kennel clubs have different rules. The Fédération Cynologique Internationale (FCI), which covers many European countries, now recognizes the long coat German Shepherd as a distinct variety. This allows them to compete in FCI-sanctioned conformation events. This difference in recognition can be confusing for new owners.

### AKC and FCI Standards

The AKC German Shepherd Dog standard specifies a "double coat of medium length." It states that "long, soft, open coats are undesirable." This means long coat German Shepherds cannot win AKC conformation titles. They can still participate in performance events like obedience or agility. They are still purebred.

The FCI, however, made a significant change. In 2011, they officially separated the German Shepherd Dog into two varieties: the "stock coat" and the "long coat." Both are now judged independently. This change reflects the popularity and acceptance of long coat German Shepherds in Europe. Understanding these standards is important for potential owners.

### Breeding for Coat Type

Breeders who aim for AKC conformation often avoid breeding long coat German Shepherds. They prioritize the stock coat. However, some breeders specialize in long coats. They focus on maintaining the breed's temperament and health, alongside the desired coat length. These breeders play a vital role in preserving the long coat variety.

Long coat varieties are often bred for companionship or performance. They are not less "German Shepherd" simply because of their fur. They still embody the breed's intelligence and loyalty. If you are interested in acquiring a German Shepherd, remember to research breeders. Ensure they prioritize health and temperament.

## FAQ Section

### Are long coat German Shepherds purebred?
Yes, long coat German Shepherds are purebred. They are a recognized variation of the German Shepherd Dog breed. Their long coat comes from inheriting a specific recessive gene from both parents. This means two standard coat German Shepherds can produce long coat puppies if they are both carriers.

### Do long coat German Shepherds shed more than stock coats?
Long coat German Shepherds often appear to shed more due to the sheer volume and length of their hair. However, they may not necessarily shed more hair by weight than stock coats. Their long fur is more noticeable when it sheds. Regular brushing is crucial to manage this shedding.

### Are long coat German Shepherds recognized by the AKC for show?
No, the American Kennel Club (AKC) does not recognize the long coat German Shepherd for conformation shows. The AKC breed standard specifies a medium-length double coat. Dogs with long coats are considered a fault in the show ring. However, they can still compete in other AKC events.

### Do long coat German Shepherds have different temperaments?
No, the coat length does not affect the temperament of a German Shepherd. Long coat German Shepherds share the same intelligent, loyal, and protective traits as stock coat German Shepherds. Their personality is determined by genetics, training, and socialization, not their fur.

### Are long coat German Shepherds healthier than stock coats?
The coat length itself does not inherently make a German Shepherd healthier or unhealthier. Both long coat and stock coat German Shepherds are susceptible to similar breed-specific health issues. Responsible breeding practices, including health screenings, are key for both types. Regular vet check-ups are important.

### Is a long coat German Shepherd good for cold weather?
A long coat German Shepherd's ability to tolerate cold weather depends on whether they have an undercoat. If they have a dense undercoat, like a stock coat, they can withstand cold well. If their long coat lacks a substantial undercoat, they may be less insulated than stock coats in very cold conditions.
