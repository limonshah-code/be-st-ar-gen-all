## Your Guide to Cleaning Hokas in the Washing Machine

Your Hoka shoes are more than just footwear; they are a vital part of your active life. They provide comfort, support, and performance for miles of running, walking, or hiking. Over time, all that activity brings dirt, sweat, and grime. A deep clean becomes essential to keep them performing their best and looking fresh.

Many people wonder if washing machines are safe for Hoka shoes. The good news is yes, you can clean Hokas in a washing machine, but with certain precautions. A proper machine wash removes stubborn dirt, eliminates odors, and extends the life of your beloved Hokas. This guide will walk you through every step. We cover everything from initial preparations to the right wash settings and careful drying.

### Takeaway: Keep Your Hokas Fresh with a Gentle Machine Wash

*   **Prepare Thoroughly:** Remove laces and insoles; brush off loose dirt.
*   **Pre-Treat Stains:** Address specific spots before the main wash.
*   **Protect Your Shoes:** Place Hokas in a mesh laundry bag with old towels.
*   **Use Gentle Settings:** Opt for cold water and a delicate or gentle cycle.
*   **Choose Mild Detergent:** Always use a gentle, liquid laundry detergent.
*   **Air Dry Completely:** Never use a dryer; allow shoes to air dry naturally.

To clean Hokas in a washing machine, remove laces and insoles. Pre-treat any tough stains. Place your shoes inside a mesh laundry bag. Wash them with cold water on a delicate or gentle cycle, using a mild liquid detergent. Always air dry your Hoka shoes thoroughly away from direct heat or sunlight.

## Essential Preparations Before Washing Your Hoka Shoes

Proper preparation is the most critical step in machine washing your Hoka shoes. Skipping these initial stages can lead to less effective cleaning or even damage. Think of this as getting your shoes ready for a spa day. You want them to be as prepared as possible for their deep clean.

### Remove Laces and Insoles

First, take out the laces from both shoes. Laces can get tangled during the wash, potentially causing damage to the machine or themselves. They also prevent water and detergent from reaching parts of the shoe under the laces. Wash laces separately by hand or place them in a small mesh bag. Next, remove the insoles. Insoles often hold a lot of sweat and odor. Washing them separately allows for a more thorough clean. You can hand wash them with mild soap and water. Allow them to air dry completely before putting them back into the shoes.

### Scrape Off Loose Dirt and Debris

Before putting your Hokas into the washing machine, remove any large chunks of dirt, mud, or debris. Use an old toothbrush, a soft brush, or even a dull knife to gently scrape away surface grime from the outsole and sides. Pay special attention to the deep treads where dirt often gets trapped. This step prevents large debris from clogging your washing machine or redepositing dirt onto the shoes during the wash cycle. It also makes the detergent more effective on the remaining stains.

### Pre-Treat Stubborn Stains

Some stains require a little extra attention before the machine wash. For specific spots like grass stains, mud streaks, or general grime, use a mild stain remover or a mixture of laundry detergent and water. Apply the solution directly to the stain. Gently scrub it with a soft brush or cloth. Let it sit for about 10-15 minutes. This allows the pre-treatment to break down the stain. This step is especially important if you have lighter-colored Hokas, such as [cleaning white Hoka shoes](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-white-hokas). Pre-treating ensures a more uniform and effective cleaning result. Rinse off any excess pre-treatment before placing the shoes in the machine.

## Choosing the Right Detergent and Laundry Accessories

Selecting the correct products for machine washing your Hokas is just as important as the preparation. The wrong choice can lead to damage, discoloration, or simply an ineffective clean. I always aim for gentle products that protect the shoe's materials.

### Mild Liquid Detergent is Best

Always use a mild liquid laundry detergent. Avoid powdered detergents, as they may not dissolve completely and can leave residue on your shoes. Harsh detergents, strong stain removers, or bleach can damage the delicate fabrics, foam, and adhesives in your Hoka shoes. Bleach, in particular, can cause yellowing on white shoes and weaken shoe materials over time. I recommend using a small amount of detergent. About half of what you would use for a regular load of laundry is usually enough for a pair of shoes. Less is more when it comes to cleaning delicate items like athletic shoes.

### The Importance of a Mesh Laundry Bag

A mesh laundry bag is an essential accessory for machine washing shoes. Place each shoe in a separate bag or both shoes in one large bag. The bag protects your shoes from direct contact with the washing machine drum. This prevents scuffs, snags, and other potential damage during agitation. It also prevents the laces or other small parts from getting tangled in the machine's moving parts. A mesh bag also helps contain any loose debris that might escape during the wash. This keeps your washing machine cleaner.

### Using Old Towels to Balance the Load

Adding a few old towels to the washing machine along with your Hokas serves multiple purposes. First, the towels help balance the load in the washing machine. This prevents the machine from becoming unbalanced during the spin cycle. Unbalanced loads can cause excessive shaking and noise, and can even damage your machine. Second, the towels provide a gentle cushioning effect for your shoes. They reduce the impact of the shoes tumbling against the machine drum and each other. Use two or three medium-sized towels. Make sure they are old ones, as some dirt from the shoes might transfer to them.

## The Washing Machine Settings for Hoka Footwear

Setting your washing machine correctly is crucial for a safe and effective clean. Incorrect settings can cause irreversible damage to your Hoka shoes. We want to clean the shoes, not destroy them. Following these guidelines will protect the integrity of your footwear.

### Cold Water: Your Best Friend for Shoe Care

Always use cold water when washing your Hoka shoes. Hot water can cause several problems. It can melt or warp the adhesives that hold your shoes together. It can also shrink or damage the synthetic fabrics and foam in the shoes. Cold water, on the other hand, is gentle on materials. It still effectively removes dirt and grime, especially when combined with a good detergent. Using cold water also saves energy, making it an eco-friendly choice. This is a non-negotiable step for preserving your Hokas.

### Delicate or Gentle Cycle Selection

Choose the delicate or gentle cycle on your washing machine. These cycles use lower agitation and a slower spin speed compared to regular cycles. High agitation can cause the shoes to bang around excessively, leading to structural damage. A slow spin cycle reduces the amount of stress on the shoe materials. It also prevents excessive water from being squeezed out, which can be hard on the shoe's shape. Some machines have a specific "hand wash" or "sneaker" cycle, which would be ideal. If not, "delicate" or "gentle" is the next best option. This careful approach is key to [cleaning sneakers in a washing machine](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-sneakers-in-the-washing-machine) successfully.

### Spin Speed: Lower is Always Better

After the wash cycle, your machine will typically go into a spin cycle to remove excess water. For Hokas, select the lowest spin speed available, or even better, no spin at all if your machine offers that option. A high spin speed can cause the shoes to be thrown around violently inside the drum. This can damage the shoes' structure and even your machine. My personal preference is to skip the spin cycle entirely if possible. This means the shoes will come out wetter, but it is much safer for them. I will then gently press out excess water by hand. If a spin cycle is unavoidable, choose the absolute lowest setting.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Washing Hokas in Your Machine

Now that we have covered all the preparations and settings, let's go through the complete process. Following these steps ensures your Hoka shoes get a thorough and safe cleaning. I find a systematic approach makes the task easy and less prone to errors.

1.  **Prepare Your Shoes:**
    *   Remove laces and insoles from both Hoka shoes. Set them aside to clean separately.
    *   Use a brush or old cloth to scrape off any loose dirt, mud, or debris from the soles and uppers.
    *   Apply a small amount of mild liquid detergent or a dedicated stain remover to any visible stains. Gently rub the area with a soft brush or your fingers. Let it sit for 10-15 minutes.

2.  **Place Shoes in Mesh Bag:**
    *   Carefully place each Hoka shoe into a separate mesh laundry bag. If your bag is large enough for both, ensure they have some space to move without excessive rubbing. This protects the shoes and prevents them from banging against the drum.

3.  **Load the Washing Machine:**
    *   Place the mesh bags with your Hoka shoes into the washing machine drum.
    *   Add 2-3 old bath towels to the load. These towels help balance the machine and provide cushioning for the shoes, reducing impact during the cycle.

4.  **Add Detergent:**
    *   Pour a small amount of mild liquid laundry detergent into the detergent dispenser. Use about half the amount you would for a regular load of clothes. Avoid using fabric softener, as it can leave residue on the shoes.

5.  **Select Wash Settings:**
    *   Set your washing machine to the "Delicate" or "Gentle" cycle.
    *   Choose "Cold Water" for the temperature setting.
    *   Select the lowest spin speed possible, or ideally, a "No Spin" option if available. This minimizes agitation and stress on the shoes.

6.  **Start the Cycle:**
    *   Close the washing machine door and start the wash cycle. Allow the machine to complete the cycle.

7.  **Remove and Inspect:**
    *   Once the cycle finishes, immediately remove your Hoka shoes from the mesh bags and the washing machine.
    *   Inspect them for cleanliness. If any stubborn spots remain, you can gently hand wash them with a soft brush and detergent, but generally, a good machine wash should handle most grime. Keeping your washing machine drum clean helps ensure that no residual dirt or detergent from previous washes gets onto your shoes. ## Proper Drying Techniques for Machine-Washed Hoka Shoes

Drying your Hoka shoes correctly after a wash is just as vital as the washing process itself. Incorrect drying methods can cause irreparable damage, shrinking, or even promote mold growth. My golden rule for Hoka drying is simple: air dry, always.

### Air Dry Only: No Dryers, No Direct Heat

Never put your Hoka shoes in a clothes dryer. The high heat from a dryer can cause the shoe materials to warp, shrink, or melt. It can also weaken the adhesives that hold the shoe together, leading to premature breakdown. The outsole might separate from the midsole, or the upper fabric could become distorted. Direct sunlight also carries similar risks. Intense UV rays and heat can fade colors and degrade materials over time. Always choose air drying as the only method for your Hokas.

### Optimal Air Drying Environment

Find a well-ventilated area indoors or in a shaded outdoor spot. This could be a laundry room, a garage, or even a porch with good airflow. Avoid damp, enclosed spaces like basements, as they can encourage mold and mildew growth. Place your shoes upright or slightly propped up to allow air to circulate around them. You can use a shoe tree or simply lean them against a wall. Make sure the area is not humid. Good air circulation is key to efficient and safe drying.

### Stuffing for Shape and Moisture Absorption

To help your Hokas retain their shape and speed up the drying process, stuff them with crumpled paper towels or old newspaper. This material absorbs excess moisture from inside the shoe. It also helps the shoe maintain its original form as it dries. Change the paper towels every few hours if they become very damp. Continue replacing them until they no longer absorb much moisture. Avoid using colored newspaper if your shoes are light-colored, as the ink can transfer. White paper towels are always a safe bet. This simple step makes a big difference in both drying time and maintaining the shoe's integrity. Depending on humidity levels, your Hokas might take 24 to 48 hours to dry completely. Patience is a virtue here. Ensure they are completely dry before wearing them again or storing them. Wearing damp shoes can lead to blisters, discomfort, and foot odor.

## Maintaining Your Hokas: Tips for Longevity and Freshness

Cleaning your Hokas thoroughly is a great step. However, regular maintenance practices between washes will significantly extend their lifespan and keep them fresh. Think of it as preventative care for your feet's best friends. These simple habits make a big difference over time.

### Regular Spot Cleaning Between Washes

You do not need to machine wash your Hokas after every single use. For minor dirt or scuffs, spot cleaning is often sufficient. Use a soft brush or a damp cloth to wipe away surface grime. A mild soap solution can tackle small stains. This quick attention prevents dirt from setting in and becoming harder to remove later. Regularly inspecting your shoes and addressing small issues immediately keeps them looking good. It also reduces the frequency of full machine washes. This gentler approach helps preserve the shoe materials over a longer period.

### Managing Odor and Moisture

Foot odor is a common issue with athletic shoes. To combat this, remove your insoles after each run or walk. Allow both the insoles and the shoes to air out completely. This helps evaporate sweat and prevents bacterial growth. You can also sprinkle baking soda inside your shoes overnight to absorb odors. Simply shake it out in the morning. Cedar shoe trees are another excellent option. They absorb moisture and leave a fresh scent. Proper ventilation and moisture control are key to keeping your Hokas smelling fresh. Avoid leaving damp shoes in a gym bag or enclosed space.

### Proper Storage for Long-Term Care

How you store your Hokas matters when you are not wearing them. Store them in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight or extreme temperatures. Avoid leaving them in a hot car or a damp basement. Extreme heat can degrade adhesives and materials. Dampness can lead to mold and mildew. Keep them in a well-ventilated area. If storing for an extended period, stuff them with acid-free paper to help maintain their shape. This attention to storage detail helps prevent material breakdown and maintains the structural integrity of your Hoka shoes.

### Knowing When to Retire Your Hokas

Even with the best cleaning and maintenance, running shoes have a finite lifespan. Typically, athletic shoes like Hokas last 300-500 miles, or about 4-6 months of regular use. Pay attention to signs of wear:
*   **Flattened Midsole:** The cushioning will feel less springy.
*   **Worn Outsole:** The tread will lose its grip.
*   **Upper Damage:** Holes or tears in the fabric.
*   **Persistent Pain:** If you start experiencing new aches or pains in your feet, knees, or hips, it might be a sign your shoes are no longer providing adequate support.

Replacing your Hokas when they are worn out is crucial for preventing injuries and maintaining performance. A clean shoe is a happy shoe, but a worn-out shoe needs replacement.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Cleaning Hokas in a Washing Machine

### Can I put Hoka insoles in the washing machine?
No, it is best to hand wash Hoka insoles. They are typically made of foam and can get damaged or lose their shape in a washing machine. Gently scrub them with mild soap and water. Rinse thoroughly and allow them to air dry completely before returning them to your shoes.

### What if my Hokas are white? Do they need special care?
White Hokas can be cleaned in the washing machine using the same methods. However, pre-treating specific stains is even more critical for white shoes. Avoid using bleach, as it can cause yellowing. A paste of baking soda and a mild detergent can help lift stubborn dirt from white areas before washing.

### How often should I wash my Hokas in the washing machine?
Machine washing should be done sparingly, perhaps every few months or when shoes are heavily soiled and odor-filled. For routine cleanliness, spot cleaning and airing out your shoes after each use are usually sufficient. Over-washing can put unnecessary wear and tear on the shoes.

### Can I use hot water to clean my Hokas for a deeper clean?
No, never use hot water when washing Hokas. Hot water can damage the shoe's adhesives, cause materials to shrink, or warp the foam cushioning. Always stick to cold water on a delicate cycle to protect the integrity and lifespan of your Hoka shoes.

### Is it safe to put my Hoka shoes in the dryer after washing?
Absolutely not. You must never put Hoka shoes in a clothes dryer. The high heat will almost certainly melt adhesives, deform the foam, and shrink the fabric. Always air dry your Hoka shoes by stuffing them with paper towels and placing them in a well-ventilated area away from direct heat or sunlight.

### What kind of detergent is best for washing Hoka shoes?
A mild liquid laundry detergent is best for washing Hoka shoes. Avoid harsh chemicals, bleach, or powdered detergents. Use only a small amount, about half of what you would use for a regular laundry load. This ensures a gentle yet effective clean without damaging materials.
