## Revitalize Your Patio: How To Clean Outdoor Wicker Furniture

Is your beautiful outdoor wicker furniture losing its vibrant look? Over time, dust, pollen, dirt, and even mildew can build up, making your patio pieces appear dull and old. I understand how frustrating it feels to see your outdoor living space look less than perfect. Regular cleaning not only restores the beauty of your furniture but also extends its lifespan.

In this guide, I will show you exactly how to clean outdoor wicker furniture. You will learn about the different types of wicker, the essential tools you need, and simple step-by-step cleaning methods. We will also cover how to tackle common issues like mold and stubborn stains. My goal is to give you all the information you need to keep your wicker looking new for years to come.

### Takeaway:
*   Identify your wicker type: natural (rattan, bamboo) or synthetic (resin, PVC).
*   Gather simple tools: vacuum, soft brush, mild soap, water, vinegar.
*   Clean regularly to prevent build-up and deep clean seasonally.
*   Address mold, mildew, and stains with appropriate, gentle solutions.
*   Protect and store wicker properly to extend its life.

### Clean outdoor wicker furniture by first removing loose debris with a vacuum or soft brush. Then, gently wash it with a mild soap and water solution, or a vinegar solution for mildew. Rinse thoroughly with clean water and allow it to dry completely in the sun.

## Understanding Your Wicker: Natural vs. Synthetic Furniture

Before you start cleaning, it is important to know what kind of wicker furniture you own. Outdoor wicker comes in two main categories: natural and synthetic. Each type requires a slightly different approach to cleaning and care. Knowing your material helps you avoid damage and choose the right cleaning products.

Natural wicker is often made from plant materials like rattan, bamboo, or willow. These materials are beautiful and offer a classic look. However, they are more susceptible to moisture damage, mold, and mildew. Natural wicker can also become brittle or unravel if not cared for properly. I always recommend caution when using water on natural wicker.

Synthetic wicker, also known as resin wicker or all-weather wicker, is made from plastic materials like PVC or polyethylene. This type of wicker is very durable and resistant to weather, fading, and mildew. Synthetic wicker is much easier to clean and requires less delicate handling. Most modern outdoor furniture uses synthetic wicker for its longevity and low maintenance.

Identifying your wicker type is usually simple. Natural wicker often has visible imperfections in its weave and a natural, earthy feel. Synthetic wicker typically has a more uniform appearance and a smooth, sometimes slightly glossy, finish. Once you know your wicker type, you can proceed with the best cleaning methods for it.

## Gathering Your Tools: Essential Supplies for Wicker Cleaning

Having the right tools makes the cleaning process much easier and more effective. You do not need fancy equipment to get your outdoor wicker furniture spotless. I find that most of the necessary items are already in my home. Simple supplies work best for gentle yet thorough cleaning.

Here is a list of the essential supplies you will need:
*   **Vacuum cleaner with brush attachment:** This helps remove loose dirt and debris from crevices.
*   **Soft-bristled brush or old toothbrush:** These are perfect for scrubbing the woven patterns without causing damage.
*   **Clean cloths or sponges:** You will use these for washing and rinsing.
*   **Two buckets:** One for your cleaning solution and one for rinse water.
*   **Mild dish soap:** A few drops are usually enough for a cleaning solution.
*   **White vinegar:** This is excellent for tackling mildew and provides a natural cleaning boost. You can learn more about using vinegar for cleaning furniture in general [How to Clean Wood Furniture with Vinegar](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-wood-furniture-with-vinegar).
*   **Spray bottle:** This helps apply cleaning solutions evenly and rinse hard-to-reach areas.
*   **Garden hose with a spray nozzle (gentle setting):** For rinsing larger pieces of synthetic wicker.
*   **Towels or old blankets:** For drying the furniture.

Avoid harsh chemicals like bleach unless specifically recommended for synthetic wicker and diluted. Bleach can damage natural wicker and fade colors. Using gentle, natural solutions protects your furniture and keeps it looking great. Gather all your supplies before you begin, so you can work efficiently.

## The Gentle Approach: Basic Cleaning Steps for Outdoor Wicker

A basic cleaning routine keeps your wicker furniture looking fresh without much effort. This method works well for light dirt and regular maintenance. I always start with a gentle approach to protect the woven material. You will be surprised how much difference these simple steps make.

First, prepare your workspace. Move your wicker furniture to an area where you can make a mess and let water run freely. A patio, driveway, or grassy area works well. Remove any cushions, pillows, or decorative items from the furniture. If your cushions are dirty, you might need to clean them separately. For mildewed cushions, consider checking out this guide on [How to Clean Mildew Off Outdoor Cushions](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-mildew-off-outdoor-cushions).

Next, remove all loose dirt and debris. Use your vacuum cleaner with a brush attachment to suction dust and crumbs from the weave. A soft-bristled brush or a stiff-bristled broom can also sweep away cobwebs and leaves. Pay close attention to the crevices where dirt tends to accumulate. Getting rid of dry debris first makes the wet cleaning step much more effective.

Prepare your cleaning solution. Mix a few drops of mild dish soap into a bucket of warm water. For a natural alternative, or if you suspect light mildew, mix equal parts white vinegar and water in a spray bottle. Always use a mild solution; strong soaps can leave residue or damage the wicker. I find this simple mix very effective for most cleaning jobs.

Now, it is time to wash the furniture. Dip a soft cloth or sponge into your cleaning solution. Gently wipe down all surfaces of the wicker. For stubborn spots or in between the weaves, use a soft-bristled brush or an old toothbrush. Scrub gently in small sections, working the solution into the weave. Be careful not to saturate natural wicker.

Finally, rinse the furniture thoroughly. For synthetic wicker, you can use a garden hose on a gentle spray setting to rinse off the soap solution. Make sure no soapy residue remains, as it can attract more dirt. For natural wicker, use a clean, damp cloth to wipe away the soap. Avoid soaking natural wicker. After rinsing, let the furniture air dry completely in a sunny spot. Ensure all moisture is gone to prevent mold growth.

## Deep Cleaning Techniques: Restoring Heavily Soiled Wicker

Sometimes, your outdoor wicker furniture needs more than just a basic wipe-down. Heavy soil, caked-on dirt, or significant grime calls for a more thorough deep cleaning. This process takes a bit more time but will bring your furniture back to life. I recommend doing a deep clean at least once or twice a year.

Start by preparing the area and removing any loose debris, just as you would for a basic clean. Use a vacuum with a hose attachment or a stiff brush to get into all the nooks and crannies. This step is crucial for removing embedded dirt before introducing water. You want to avoid turning dry grime into muddy streaks.

For heavily soiled synthetic wicker, you can create a slightly stronger cleaning solution. Mix about 1/4 cup of mild dish soap into a gallon of warm water. You can also add 1 cup of white vinegar for extra cleaning power, especially if there is a musty smell. For very tough spots on synthetic wicker, a very diluted bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) can be used, but always spot-test first in an inconspicuous area. I rarely find this necessary.

Apply the cleaning solution generously to the synthetic wicker using a sponge or a spray bottle. Let the solution sit on the furniture for 5-10 minutes. This allows the soap to break down the dirt and grime. While it is soaking, use a soft-bristled brush or a scrub brush to gently agitate the surface. Work the brush into the weave, paying extra attention to areas with heavy build-up. You will see the dirt start to lift away.

For natural wicker that is heavily soiled, avoid excessive water. Instead, use a damp cloth dipped in a mild soap and water solution. Gently wipe and scrub sections. You can use a soft brush for tougher spots, but do not drench the material. If the natural wicker is extremely dirty, consider professional cleaning or very careful spot cleaning with specialized natural wicker cleaners.

After scrubbing, rinse the synthetic wicker thoroughly with a garden hose on a gentle setting. Make sure all soap residue is completely gone. Residual soap can attract new dirt and leave a dull film. For natural wicker, use several clean, damp cloths to wipe away the solution until no soap remains. Then, use dry towels to blot as much moisture as possible from the natural wicker. Always allow both types of wicker to air dry completely in direct sunlight. This step is critical to prevent mold or mildew growth. Prop the furniture up if possible to allow air circulation underneath.

## Tackling Troubles: Removing Mold, Mildew, and Stubborn Stains

Mold and mildew are common problems for outdoor furniture, especially in damp climates. These fungi not only look unsightly but can also damage your wicker over time. Stubborn stains from food, drinks, or rust also need specific attention. I have found effective ways to tackle these issues and restore wicker's clean look.

For mold and mildew on **synthetic wicker**, a solution of white vinegar and water works wonders. Mix equal parts white vinegar and water in a spray bottle. Spray the affected areas generously. Let it sit for about 15-20 minutes. The vinegar will kill the mold and mildew spores. Then, scrub the area with a soft-bristled brush. Rinse thoroughly with a garden hose and let it dry in the sun. For very stubborn mold on synthetic wicker, you can use a diluted bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water). Apply it with a sponge, scrub, and rinse quickly. Always wear gloves and eye protection when using bleach.

When dealing with mold or mildew on **natural wicker**, avoid bleach, as it can damage the material. Instead, use a solution of 1/2 cup white vinegar mixed with 1 gallon of warm water. Dampen a cloth with this solution and gently wipe the moldy areas. You can use a soft brush to lightly scrub the spots. Immediately after, use a clean, damp cloth to rinse the area and then blot dry with a towel. Ensure the natural wicker dries completely. You can place it in a sunny, airy spot to help it dry faster. Preventing mildew is key; regularly cleaning and ensuring furniture dries quickly helps. This is similar to how you would approach mildew on other outdoor items like [How to Clean Outdoor Rug with Mildew](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-outdoor-rug-with-mildew). For more general guidance on mold, you might also find this article helpful: [How to Clean Mold Off of Wood Furniture](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-mold-off-of-wood-furniture).

For **grease or oil stains**, sprinkle cornstarch or baking soda directly onto the stain. Let it sit for several hours or overnight. This absorbent powder will soak up the oil. Then, brush off the powder. Follow up with your mild soap and water solution, scrubbing gently. Rinse and dry as usual.

**Food or drink stains** can often be removed with a simple mild soap and water solution. For tougher stains like wine or coffee, a paste of baking soda and a little water can be applied. Let it sit for a few minutes before gently scrubbing and rinsing. Always test any stain removal method on an inconspicuous area first to ensure it does not damage or discolor the wicker. Prompt action on spills prevents stains from setting in.

## Protecting Your Investment: Post-Cleaning Care and Maintenance

Cleaning your outdoor wicker furniture is just the first step. Proper care after cleaning and regular maintenance will keep it looking great and extend its life significantly. I believe that prevention is always better than cure when it comes to furniture upkeep. A little effort goes a long way.

Once your wicker furniture is sparkling clean and completely dry, consider applying a protectant. For **synthetic wicker**, a UV protectant spray can help prevent fading and degradation from sun exposure. These sprays often contain silicones that create a barrier against the elements. Follow the product instructions carefully for application. This adds an extra layer of defense against the harsh outdoor environment.

For **natural wicker**, applying a furniture polish or a clear sealer designed for natural materials can help nourish the fibers and protect them from moisture. Always choose products specifically for natural wood or wicker. These protectants help maintain the flexibility of the natural fibers, preventing them from becoming brittle and breaking. I find that natural wicker needs this extra care to stay supple.

Regular light cleaning between deep cleans is crucial. I make it a habit to quickly dust or vacuum my wicker furniture once a week or every other week. This prevents dust, pollen, and light debris from building up and becoming ingrained in the weave. A quick wipe-down with a damp cloth can also refresh the surface. This routine saves you from needing frequent deep cleaning.

Consider using outdoor furniture covers, especially during periods of heavy rain, strong sun, or when the furniture is not in use for extended periods. High-quality covers protect your wicker from environmental damage, including UV rays, moisture, and dirt. They are an excellent investment for preserving your outdoor pieces. You can find useful tips on using and cleaning these covers in this article: [How to Clean Outdoor Furniture Covers](https://beacleaner.com/how-to-clean-outdoor-furniture-covers). Protecting your furniture with covers significantly reduces the need for frequent intensive cleaning.

## Seasonal Storage: Preserving Wicker Furniture Off-Season

Proper storage during the off-season, especially winter or prolonged periods of non-use, is vital for the longevity of your outdoor wicker furniture. Cold temperatures, snow, and excessive moisture can cause significant damage. I always recommend preparing your furniture for storage to ensure it stays in excellent condition.

Before storing, give your wicker furniture a thorough deep clean. Follow all the steps for removing dirt, mold, mildew, and stains. Make sure the furniture is absolutely, completely dry before putting it away. Storing damp wicker, especially natural wicker, encourages mold and mildew growth, and can cause natural fibers to rot. I cannot stress enough the importance of complete dryness.

Choose a suitable storage location. The best place for outdoor wicker furniture is a dry, cool, and well-ventilated area. A garage, shed, or basement is ideal. Avoid storing wicker directly on concrete floors, which can transfer moisture. You can place wood pallets or tarps underneath to create a barrier. If indoor storage is not possible, a protected porch or covered patio can work, but ensure the furniture is fully covered.

Protect your wicker with breathable furniture covers. While heavy-duty covers are great for outdoor protection, for long-term storage, a breathable cover helps prevent moisture buildup and mildew. If using plastic tarps, ensure there is some airflow to prevent condensation. Secure the covers tightly to prevent wind from dislodging them.

For natural wicker, consider adding a few silica gel packets or moisture absorbers inside the covers or near the furniture during storage. These will help absorb any lingering humidity. Inspect your stored furniture periodically, especially if your storage area experiences humidity fluctuations. Just a quick check can prevent unexpected issues.

If your outdoor wicker furniture includes cushions, store them separately. Clean and dry your cushions thoroughly before storing them in airtight bags or containers. This prevents mildew and keeps them fresh for the next season. By taking these extra steps for seasonal storage, you will ensure your wicker furniture remains a beautiful and inviting part of your outdoor space for many years. ## FAQ Section

### Can I pressure wash wicker furniture?
I do not recommend pressure washing wicker furniture. The high pressure can damage the woven material, especially natural wicker, by unraveling fibers or forcing water too deeply into the material. For synthetic wicker, it might be possible on a very low setting, but it is still risky. A gentle hose spray or a bucket of water works best.

### How often should I clean my outdoor wicker?
I suggest a light cleaning (dusting, wiping) weekly or bi-weekly during peak season. A more thorough deep clean is ideal at least once a season, typically in spring or before storing it away for winter. Regular quick cleans prevent major build-up and make deep cleaning easier.

### What's the best way to clean white wicker?
White wicker shows dirt more easily. Use the same mild soap and water solution or vinegar solution as for other wicker types. Pay extra attention to scrubbing crevices gently with a soft brush. For stubborn stains or yellowing on synthetic white wicker, a highly diluted bleach solution (1:10 bleach to water) can be used, but always spot-test first. Rinse thoroughly.

### Can I use bleach on wicker?
You can use a highly diluted bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) on **synthetic wicker** for stubborn mold or stains. Always spot-test first. I do not recommend using bleach on **natural wicker** as it can cause damage, discoloration, and weaken the fibers. Stick to vinegar or mild soap for natural materials.

### How do I remove stubborn stains?
For stubborn stains on wicker, first identify the stain type (grease, food, mold). For grease, apply cornstarch or baking soda, let it absorb, then brush off. For food/drink, use a baking soda paste. For mold, a vinegar solution is excellent. Always pre-test any stain removal method on an inconspicuous area of your wicker furniture.

### My wicker is unraveling. Can I fix it?
For synthetic wicker, unraveling is rare but can sometimes be rewoven or glued with a strong outdoor adhesive if it's a loose end. For natural wicker, unraveling can be a sign of age or damage. Small unravels can sometimes be tucked back in and secured with a dab of waterproof wood glue. Major unraveling might require professional repair or replacement.
