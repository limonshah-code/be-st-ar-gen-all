## Understanding Bernese Mountain Dog Growth Stages

## Physical Maturity: When Your Bernese Reaches Full Size

The physical development of a Bernese Mountain Dog is a long process. Most of their vertical growth, meaning their height, slows down significantly by 12 to 18 months. However, their body continues to broaden and add muscle. This filling out phase can last until they are 3 or even 4 years old.

Adult Bernese Mountain Dogs are substantial in size. Males typically weigh between 80 to 115 pounds and stand 25 to 27.5 inches tall at the shoulder. Females are slightly smaller, weighing 70 to 95 pounds and standing 23 to 26 inches tall. These measurements represent their full adult size. It is important to remember that individual dogs can vary within these ranges.

Bone plate closure is a key part of physical maturity. These are areas of cartilage in growing bones. They harden into bone as the dog matures. For large breeds like the Bernese, these growth plates usually close between 18 to 24 months. Once growth plates close, bones no longer lengthen. This means their height will not increase significantly after this point.

During their growth, Bernese puppies experience growth spurts. They might seem to grow overnight, followed by periods of slower growth. Their legs might look too long for their body, creating an awkward adolescent appearance. This is a normal part of their development. Understanding these stages helps you manage their care, preventing injuries to their developing joints. ## Factors Influencing Bernese Mountain Dog Growth

Several factors play a significant role in how and when a Bernese Mountain Dog reaches its full size. Understanding these influences can help you support healthy growth. Each element contributes to your dog's development. My focus is always on giving them the best start.

**Genetics:** This is perhaps the most important factor. A puppy's adult size and growth rate are largely determined by the size and genetic makeup of its parents. If the parents were large and slow-growing, their offspring are likely to follow a similar pattern. Responsible breeders can provide information on the parents' growth history. This gives you an idea of what to expect from your puppy.

**Nutrition:** Proper nutrition is vital for healthy growth. Bernese Mountain Dog puppies need a diet specifically formulated for large breeds. These foods contain balanced calcium and phosphorus levels. They also have a lower calorie density compared to regular puppy foods. This helps prevent too-rapid growth, which can lead to skeletal problems like hip and elbow dysplasia. Overfeeding can cause obesity and stress on developing joints.

**Exercise:** Appropriate exercise is crucial. While puppies need activity, over-exercising a growing Bernese can be harmful. Intense, high-impact activities like long runs or jumping should be avoided until their growth plates have closed. Structured walks and gentle play are more suitable. This protects their vulnerable joints during this critical growth period.

**Overall Health:** A puppy's health status affects its growth. Illnesses, parasites, or chronic conditions can hinder normal development. Regular veterinary check-ups are essential to monitor growth and address any health concerns promptly. A healthy puppy is a growing puppy.

**Spaying/Neutering:** The timing of spaying or neutering can also influence bone growth. Early spaying or neutering, especially before growth plates close, can delay their closure. This can lead to slightly taller dogs with altered bone density. Many veterinarians now recommend waiting until large breeds are closer to physical maturity (18-24 months) before these procedures. This allows for proper bone development.

## Monitoring Your Bernese Mountain Dog's Development

Monitoring your Bernese Mountain Dog's growth is essential. It helps ensure they are developing at a healthy rate. Regular checks can catch potential issues early. I believe staying informed about their progress is a part of responsible ownership.

**Regular Vet Check-ups:** Scheduled visits to your veterinarian are important. Your vet will weigh your puppy and assess their overall body condition. They can provide growth charts specific to large breeds. This helps track their progress against expected growth curves. These visits also allow for discussions about nutrition and exercise.

**Weight and Height Tracking:** You can keep a record of your puppy's weight at home. Use a reliable scale. While tracking height is harder, you can note when they appear to hit certain milestones. Compare your dog's progress with average Bernese Mountain Dog growth charts. Remember that individual variations exist, so do not panic if your dog is slightly above or below the average.

**Observing Body Condition:** Look at your dog's body. A healthy growing Bernese should have a visible waist when viewed from above. You should be able to feel their ribs easily without seeing them. If your puppy seems too thin or too heavy, adjust their diet under vet guidance. Too much weight puts strain on their growing joints.

**Recognizing Developmental Milestones:** Pay attention to behavioral changes as well. Note when they start exhibiting more adult-like behaviors. This includes increased calmness or independence. Observe how they move. Any limping, stiffness, or reluctance to play should prompt a vet visit. These could be signs of skeletal issues common in large breeds.

**Dental Development:** While not directly tied to size, a dog's teeth offer clues about their age and maturity. Puppies lose their baby teeth and gain adult teeth over several months. By 6-7 months, most adult teeth are in. This gives a general indicator of their early maturity. ## Caring for a Growing Bernese: Diet, Exercise, and Health

Caring for a growing Bernese Mountain Dog requires specific attention to their diet, exercise, and overall health. These large breed puppies have unique needs to support their long growth period. Providing proper care is crucial for their long-term well-being. I always prioritize their health during this critical phase.

**Specific Dietary Needs:** A large breed puppy diet is essential. These foods are designed to promote slower, more controlled growth. They contain carefully balanced levels of calcium and phosphorus. This helps prevent rapid bone growth that can lead to orthopedic problems. Avoid high-calorie regular puppy foods. They can cause a puppy to grow too quickly, stressing their developing joints. Feed measured portions and avoid free-feeding to prevent overeating.

**Appropriate Exercise:** Exercise is necessary for a Bernese puppy, but it must be controlled. Avoid strenuous activities until their growth plates have closed, usually around 18-24 months. This means no long-distance running, jumping from heights, or intense, repetitive impact activities. Instead, focus on short, frequent walks. Allow for supervised playtime in a secure area. This helps build muscle and stamina without putting undue stress on their developing skeletal system. For instance, knowing [how long it takes for a Pitbull to be full grown](https://beacleaner.com/how-long-does-it-take-for-a-pitbull-to-be-full-grown) can offer a good comparison, as Pitbulls generally mature physically faster than Bernese Mountain Dogs.

**Common Health Concerns During Growth:** Bernese Mountain Dogs are prone to certain health issues, especially during their growth phase. Hip and elbow dysplasia are common orthopedic conditions. These are influenced by genetics, diet, and exercise. Watch for limping, stiffness, or reluctance to move. Early detection and management are key. Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD) is another joint condition to be aware of. Regular vet check-ups can help monitor for these conditions.

**Preventative Care:** Vaccinations, parasite control, and regular deworming are vital. These preventative measures protect your growing puppy from common diseases and infections. A healthy puppy is more likely to develop properly. Discuss a vaccination schedule with your vet. This ensures your Bernese receives all necessary protection as they grow.

**Grooming and Socialization:** Beyond physical needs, continue with regular grooming. This gets them accustomed to handling. Consistent socialization is also important. Introduce your puppy to various people, places, and other friendly dogs. This helps them develop into a confident, well-adjusted adult Bernese.

## FAQ Section

### Q1: Do Bernese Mountain Dogs grow slowly?
Yes, Bernese Mountain Dogs are a large breed and grow relatively slowly compared to smaller dogs. They reach their full height by 18-24 months but continue to fill out in weight and muscle until 3-4 years of age. Their mental maturity also lags, often developing fully by 2-3 years. This extended growth period requires patience from owners.

### Q2: What is the average weight of a full grown Bernese?
A full-grown male Bernese Mountain Dog typically weighs between 80 to 115 pounds. Females are slightly lighter, usually weighing 70 to 95 pounds. These weights represent a healthy range for adult dogs. Individual variations can occur based on genetics and overall health.

### Q3: When do Bernese Mountain Dogs calm down?
Bernese Mountain Dogs usually start to calm down and exhibit more adult behaviors around 2 to 3 years of age. While they may reach physical maturity earlier, their mental and emotional maturity takes longer. Consistent training and socialization during their adolescent phase help them transition to a calm adult temperament.

### Q4: Does spaying/neutering affect Bernese growth?
Yes, the timing of spaying or neutering can affect a Bernese Mountain Dog's growth. Performing these procedures before growth plates close (around 18-24 months) can potentially delay plate closure. This may result in slightly longer bones and altered bone density. Many vets recommend waiting until physical maturity for large breeds.

### Q5: What should I feed my growing Bernese Mountain Dog?
You should feed your growing Bernese Mountain Dog a high-quality commercial dog food specifically formulated for large breed puppies. These diets are designed with controlled calorie levels and balanced minerals like calcium and phosphorus. This promotes slower, healthy bone growth and helps prevent orthopedic issues. Always follow feeding guidelines and consult your vet.

### Q6: How can I tell if my Bernese is done growing?
You can tell your Bernese is likely done growing in height when they are around 18-24 months old. Their weight may continue to increase as they fill out their frame until 3-4 years of age. A veterinarian can confirm growth plate closure with X-rays. Observing their energy levels and temperament becoming more consistently calm also indicates mental maturity.
